📚 2. Literature Review

2.1 Background & Domain Knowledge

Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) remain the leading cause of death globally, accounting for an estimated 17.9 million deaths per year (WHO, 2021). Early detection and risk assessment are critical for reducing mortality. Machine learning approaches have shown promising results in predicting heart disease risk from behavioral and clinical data.

Key Research Findings

[1] CDC BRFSS 2020 Survey
The Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) is the largest continuously conducted health survey in the world, with 400,000+ adults interviewed annually. Our dataset (319,795 records, 48 features) covers demographics, behavioral habits, chronic conditions, and self-reported health status. The class imbalance ratio is 1:10.7 (8.56% positive for heart disease).
[2] LightGBM for Medical Classification (Ke et al., 2017)
LightGBM's leaf-wise tree growth strategy and native support for class imbalance (is_unbalance=True) make it highly effective for medical datasets. Studies show LightGBM achieves comparable or superior performance to XGBoost with significantly faster training time. Our results confirm this — LightGBM V2 achieved the highest ROC-AUC (0.8404) among all individual models.
[3] SMOTE for Imbalanced Medical Data (Chawla et al., 2002)
Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) generates synthetic samples along the line segments between existing minority instances. Critical best practice: SMOTE must be applied only to training data to prevent data leakage. Our implementation follows this strictly — test data remains original and unmodified.
[4] Bayesian Hyperparameter Optimization — Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019)
Optuna's Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) provides more efficient hyperparameter search than Grid Search or Random Search. We used 50 trials per model on 50,000-sample subsets, discovering optimal parameters that improved our baseline LightGBM from 0.8378 to 0.8404 ROC-AUC.
[5] SHAP Values for Explainable AI (Lundberg & Lee, 2017)
SHapley Additive exPlanations provides theoretically grounded feature importance based on cooperative game theory. Unlike traditional feature importance, SHAP shows directional contribution — whether a feature increases or decreases risk. Our Streamlit app integrates real-time SHAP waterfall plots for individual patient explanations.
[6] Youden's J Statistic for Optimal Threshold (Youden, 1950)
In medical screening, the default 50% classification threshold is suboptimal due to asymmetric misclassification costs (missing a sick patient is far worse than a false alarm). Youden's J (J = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1) finds the ROC-optimal threshold. We discovered 49.22% as our optimal threshold, achieving 81.28% Recall.
[7] Star Schema for Healthcare Data Warehousing (Kimball, 2013)
The Star Schema design pattern — with a central fact table surrounded by dimension tables — is the gold standard for analytical data warehousing. Our implementation uses fact_Diagnosis with 6 dimension tables (Patients, HealthStatus, Conditions, Lifestyle, Geography, Date), enabling efficient Power BI reporting.

2.4 Suggested Improvements & Future Work

  1. Deep Learning Models: Explore 1D-CNN or TabNet architectures for tabular data to potentially improve AUC beyond 0.84.
  2. Real-Time EHR Integration: Connect to hospital Electronic Health Record systems via HL7 FHIR APIs for live patient data.
  3. Multi-Language Support: Extend the Streamlit app to support Arabic interface for Egyptian hospitals.
  4. Federated Learning: Train models across multiple hospitals without sharing patient data for privacy compliance.
  5. Time-Series Analysis: Track individual patient risk trajectories over multiple visits using longitudinal data.
  6. Mobile App: Develop a Flutter/React Native mobile version for field healthcare workers.
